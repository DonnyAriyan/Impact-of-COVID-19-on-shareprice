COVID-19 And It's Impact on Gross Domestic Product per capita (GDP per capita) of various countries across the globe

What is the effect of COVID-19 on a global scale?

It is undeniable that COVID-19 pandemic had presented us with new challenges such as not only social disruption but also as a profound risk to the health and growth economies of countries the globe. This can be measured by Gross Domestic Product per capita or GDP-per-capita which, in short, refers to the overall health of the economy of a particular country, measured by the total monetary value of all final goods and services produced and sold in the economy divided by the total population.




Which countries were most affected in the year, in terms of GDP-per-capita , by the COVID-19 ?

Here, we have compiled the top 5 countries most affected by COVID-19 in the year, 2020; The Bahamas, Lebanon, etc. When comparing percentage (%) change values from the heatmap for the exact same 5 countries for the previous year, 2019, we can see that, for example, Maldives was experiencing a 6.9% increase in its GDP-per-capita when compared to 2018 however, when looking at its 2020 GDP-per-capita we can observe a significant slump in its GDP-per-capita from 6.9% to -33.5%, this is a 40.4% difference. Looking at a GDP-per-capita of -33.5% individually is already shocking. This means that there is, on average, a 33.5% decrease in the economic output per person in Maldives.

What is the difference in these 5 countries' GDP-per-capita from the previous year, 2019?

When looking within the scope of the GDP-per-capita percentage (%) change we can deduce that although some of these countries were already experiencing declines in GDP-per-capita growth, it is quite unanimous that these declines in GDP-per-capita are very trivial compared to the values in 2020, reaching unprecedented levels, for example, Macao experiencing a -54% decline. It is true and important to note that although COVID-19 does explain a large extent it does not explain the full extent of this decrease as there are a multitude of other external factors such as government intervention via policy implementation or change.




Through observing Figure 1, we are able to interpret that in the year, 2020, the economy on a global scale was profoundly impacted by COVID-19 where many economies faced a huge downturn ultimately, having implications of economic turmoil and decreased productivity. We are also able to deduce that even the COVID-19 pandemic first surfaced in late 2018, economies began to suffer the full effects in 2020 where majority of the countries experienced negative percentage (%) change in GDP-per-capita (denoted by countries that are red in colour) except for Guyana which experienced a 43.5% increase in its GDP-per-capita relative to the previous year, 2019.

Here, we aim to find if there is any form
of correlation between the percentage (%) change in GDP-per-capita in 2019 between that in 2020, which can be achieved by filtering countries experiencing increases in GDP-per-capita in both years, 2019 and 2020. From observation, we can extrapolate that, in 2019, majority of the countries experienced a percentage change of 7% or below however, when looking from the percentage change of GDP-per-capita from 2020, we can see that many countries experienced a GDP-per-capita change, in percentage, of less than 5% with one country, the Congo, experiencing an approximate 43% change which is an outlier. Hence, on average, out of the countries that experienced an increase in GDP-per-capita for both years, there is significantly less of an increase in that percentage change in GDP-per-capita in the year of 2020 relative to 2019, with the majority experiencing declines in GDP-per-capita , which are filtered out of this idiom.

In this idiom, the five most COVID-19 affected countries have been regressed with the five least COVID-19 affected countries, these countries are regressed against their very own, exact GDP-per-capita for the year, 2020. Here, we can observe that the aggregate difference between the total in GDP-per-capita decreases heavily exceeds the total in the GDP-per-capita increases (in the top 5 countries that have experienced a positive GDP-per-capita growth in 2020). From this, we can conclude that COVID-19 was indeed a large factor in play in the economic crisis and turmoil that unfolded causing economies to slump and markets to crash.

What can we take out of this?

In spite of the optimism towards the future of the financial markets and the economy, COVID-19 has still challenged the world and has established itself as one of the largest risks to economic development.
